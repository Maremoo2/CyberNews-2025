# June 2025 - Norway Summary

## Overview

Norway's cybersecurity environment in June 2025 was marked by significant legislative achievement and heightened awareness following the April Bremanger dam incident, which became publicly known in late June. While the country experienced no major new cyberattacks during the month, the revelation of critical infrastructure vulnerability and the passage of comprehensive cybersecurity legislation represented a pivotal moment for Norwegian cyber defense.

The month demonstrated that Norway, despite strong cyber defenses and a relatively low number of successful breaches, remains vulnerable to both simple attacks exploiting basic security failures and sophisticated nation-state threats. The government's response – combining new legislation, practical guidance, and international cooperation – positioned Norway to significantly strengthen its cybersecurity posture heading into the second half of 2025.

## Key Trends

### Critical Infrastructure Vulnerability Exposed

The Bremanger dam incident (April 7, publicly disclosed in late June) fundamentally shifted perceptions:

**From Information Security to Physical Safety:**
The attack demonstrated that cyber operations can directly manipulate physical infrastructure with potential for casualties, transforming cybersecurity from primarily an IT concern to a clear national security and public safety issue.

**Simple but Effective:**
The fact that attackers used just a weak password – no sophisticated malware or zero-day exploits – made the incident more concerning, not less. It revealed that basic security hygiene failures exist even in critical infrastructure controlling water resources.

**Accelerated Security Projects:**
The incident prompted utility companies to accelerate projects to:
- Separate and better secure operational technology (OT) networks
- Remove unnecessary internet connectivity
- Implement multi-factor authentication
- Enhance monitoring capabilities

**PST Validation:**
The attack validated warnings from Norway's Police Security Service (PST) that Russia might target Norway's energy infrastructure as part of hybrid warfare tactics, given Norway's NATO membership and support for Ukraine.

### Legislative Milestone: Digital Security Act

The passage of the Digital Security Act on June 23, 2025 represented Norway's most significant cybersecurity legislation to date:

**EU Alignment:**
Closely aligned with EU's NIS2 directive, ensuring Norwegian standards match European best practices despite Norway not being an EU member.

**Comprehensive Coverage:**
Mandates apply to critical sectors:
- Energy (particularly relevant post-dam incident)
- Transportation
- Healthcare
- Finance
- Digital infrastructure

**Clear Requirements:**
- 24-hour incident reporting to NSM
- Board-level accountability
- Regular risk assessments
- Robust security measures
- Sector-specific enforcement

**October 2025 Implementation:**
The October 1 effective date gave organizations time to prepare while creating urgency for compliance planning.

### Russia-Ukraine War Spillover

As a close NATO ally and Europe's largest gas supplier, Norway remained a high-value target for Russian cyber operations:

**Continuous Low-Level Activity:**
Throughout spring and into June 2025, Norway faced:
- DDoS campaigns targeting government websites
- Attempts to breach oil & gas company networks
- Phishing campaigns by pro-Russian hacktivist groups
- Espionage-oriented intrusions

**Successful Defense:**
Most attempts in June were successfully defended, though they kept threat levels high and required sustained vigilance.

**Strategic Targeting:**
Attacks appeared focused on:
- Energy sector intelligence gathering
- Government communications
- Defense-related entities
- Critical infrastructure reconnaissance

### Improved Cyber Readiness and Coordination

On a positive note, Norway demonstrated significant improvements in cybersecurity capabilities:

**Agency Coordination:**
Seamless interplay between:
- PST (Police Security Service) for threat intelligence
- NSM (National Security Authority) for technical defense
- Sectoral CERTs for industry-specific response
- NVE (Water Resources Directorate) for critical infrastructure

**Proactive Defense:**
- NSM detected and quietly mitigated attempted intrusion targeting defense contractor's email server in May (using recently disclosed Exchange server flaw)
- Intelligence-led defense leveraging threat intelligence to harden potential targets ahead of attacks
- The National Cyber Security Centre (established 2019) enabled early detection of some intrusions

**Quick Investigation:**
Rapid attribution and analysis of the dam incident by summer demonstrated strong investigative capabilities and inter-agency cooperation.

### Cybercrime Landscape

Norway's cybercrime remained at lower levels compared to larger countries, but showed concerning patterns:

**Business Email Compromise:**
Norwegian businesses (especially finance and shipping) reported increased BEC and phishing activity.

**Ransomware:**
No major public incidents in June, but:
- Many SMEs likely dealt with smaller infections quietly
- Manufacturing sector reportedly affected (known anecdotally in security community)
- Global trends suggested Norway would face increased targeting

**Extortion Without Encryption:**
NorCERT observed trend of criminals threatening to leak stolen data from Norwegian firms:
- Made easier when companies have good backups
- Fear of data exposure remains effective leverage
- Demonstrates evolution of extortion tactics

### Political and Public Awareness

Cyber threats gained significant visibility in Norwegian politics and public discourse:

**Leadership Priority:**
- Cyber planned as topic for annual Arendalsuka political forum in August
- Indicated elevation to top-tier political issue

**Budget Implications:**
- The dam event likely influenced Norway's 2026 budget planning
- Increased allocation anticipated for cyber defense
- Emergency funding for critical infrastructure cybersecurity upgrades

**Media Coverage:**
- Increased public interest following dam incident
- Educational coverage of cyber threats
- Discussion of national cyber resilience

## Government Cybersecurity Initiatives

### Digital Security Act (DSA) – Passed June 23, 2025

**Timeline:**
- Preparatory work and debates through April-May
- Passed by Norwegian Storting (parliament) in late June 2025
- Effective Date: October 1, 2025

**Alignment:**
Closely aligned with EU's NIS2 directive and other European regulations, ensuring Norway maintains compatibility despite not being EU member.

**Key Requirements:**

**Covered Entities:**
- Essential services and critical businesses
- Energy, transport, healthcare, finance, digital infrastructure sectors

**Mandatory Measures:**
- Clear cybersecurity requirements
- Mandated risk assessments
- Incident reporting within 24 hours to NSM
- Board-level responsibility for cyber preparedness
- Regular security audits
- Supply chain security assessments

**Enforcement:**
- Specified fines for non-compliance
- Sector-specific regulators assigned oversight
- Graduated penalties based on severity

**Catalyst:**
Lawmakers in May's final committee review explicitly cited the dam incident as evidence for stronger cyber laws, demonstrating how real-world incidents drive policy.

**Source:** [DLA Piper](https://norway.dlapiper.com)

### NSM National Framework (Late June 2025)

NSM published a comprehensive **national framework for handling digital attacks**, reported by local media with emphasis on systematic incident response.

**Key Components:**

**Incident Management:**
- Detection and analysis procedures
- Escalation criteria and protocols
- Response coordination mechanisms
- Recovery and lessons learned processes

**Inter-Agency Cooperation:**
- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Communication protocols
- Shared threat intelligence
- Joint exercises and training

**Public-Private Collaboration:**
- Industry involvement in threat response
- Information sharing protocols
- Coordinated vulnerability disclosure
- Collective defense approach

**Integration:**
Builds on lessons from past crises and integrates with:
- EU's Cybersecurity Blueprint
- NATO cyber defense frameworks
- Scandinavian cooperation agreements

**Source:** [DataGuidance - NSM Framework](https://dataguidance.com)

### NSM Advisories

#### Global Ransomware Warning

**Content:**
- Alert on global ransomware uptick in June
- Cited trends from Europol and FBI partners
- Specific threat actor profiles (Qilin, Fog, Anubis)

**Recommendations:**
- Implement offline backups
- Conduct ransomware drills
- Segment networks
- Test recovery procedures
- Maintain updated incident response plans
- Train staff on ransomware indicators

#### 16 Billion Credential Leak Bulletin

**Risk Highlighted:**
Massive credential dump available to attackers creates risks of:
- Account takeover attacks
- Lateral movement in corporate networks
- Business email compromise
- Social engineering campaigns

**Urgent Actions:**
- Enforce organization-wide password resets
- Mandate multi-factor authentication everywhere possible
- Monitor for account takeover attempts
- Educate employees about password reuse dangers
- Implement zero trust architectures

**Rationale:**
Password reuse by employees could lead to breaches given the credential dump, making proactive measures essential.

### Energy Sector Cybersecurity Exercise (Mid-June 2025)

**Organizers:**
Facilitated by NVE (Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate) and NSM.

**Timing:**
Planned earlier but gained prominence and urgency post-Bremanger dam hack.

**Scenario:**
Simulated cyberattack on regional power grid control system, including:
- Initial compromise
- Lateral movement
- Potential physical impact
- Multi-stakeholder response

**Objectives:**
- Practice new reporting flows mandated by DSA
- Test coordination between utilities and authorities
- Validate NSM's framework
- Identify communication gaps
- Improve collective response capabilities

**Outcomes:**
- Identified gaps in communication that would be fixed before DSA implementation
- Validated incident response procedures
- Strengthened relationships between responders
- Improved understanding of cascading effects

**Participants:**
- Major power utilities
- Grid operators
- NVE regulators
- NSM experts
- Municipal energy authorities
- Emergency services

### Public Awareness Campaign

**Launch:** May 2025 (continued through June)

**Name:** "Sikkert Samfunn 2025" (Secure Society 2025)

**Channels:**
- Social media campaigns
- TV public service announcements
- Print media
- Educational materials

**Content:**
- Advice on two-factor authentication
- Identifying scam messages
- Importance of software updates
- Password hygiene
- Reporting suspicious activity

**Rationale:**
May survey showed over 60% of Norwegians worried about identity theft, demonstrating public concern and receptiveness to guidance.

**Goal:**
Reduce human-factor risks through preventative education and improved cyber hygiene across population.

### International Cooperation

#### NATO Cyber Defense

**Locked Shields 2025:**
- Norwegian participation in major NATO cyber exercise
- Testing joint responses to power grid and telecom attacks
- Practicing cross-border coordination
- Sharing lessons learned

**Information Sharing:**
- Enhanced threat intelligence exchange
- Joint threat assessments
- Coordinated attribution efforts

#### Scandinavian Cyber Agreement (Mid-May)

**Partners:** Norway, Sweden, Denmark

**Focus:** Energy sector protection in Scandinavia

**Components:**
- Real-time threat intelligence sharing
- Joint incident response protocols
- Coordinated vulnerability disclosure
- Shared security standards
- Common risk assessments

**Rationale:**
Interconnected Nordic energy grid requires coordinated cyber defense.

#### Cyber Range Funding (May 18)

**Announcement:**
Funding for new Cyber Range at Norwegian university.

**Capabilities:**
- Simulate realistic attack scenarios
- Train incident responders
- Test security technologies
- Develop domestic talent
- Support research

**Strategic Goal:**
Reduce dependence on foreign cybersecurity expertise by developing domestic capabilities.

## Statistics

### Incident Volume

**First Half 2025:**
- NSM handled approximately **500 serious cyber incidents**
- Increase from ~400 in same period 2024 (~25% increase)
- Reflects both increased targeting and improved detection

**June Specifically:**
- **Few dozen incidents** of note, most minor
- Malware infections (mostly phishing-delivered)
- DDoS attacks (primarily targeting government sites)
- Attempted network intrusions (mostly detected and blocked)

**Tier 1 Incident:**
- Bremanger dam attack was **single significant incident** in terms of potential impact
- No other critical infrastructure breaches reported

### Target Sectors (H1 2025)

Based on serious attempted breaches:

1. **Government and Defense** (40%) – Consistent with state-sponsored targeting
2. **Energy Sector** (20%) – Reflecting Norway's standing as oil, gas, and hydroelectric provider
3. **Shipping/Maritime** (10-15%) – Norwegian shipping companies faced constant phishing
4. **Finance** (10-15%) – Though none led to major breaches in June
5. **Other** (15-20%) – Including healthcare, education, technology

### Financial Impact

**May-June 2025:**
- Limited financial losses (no major ransomware payouts or prolonged stoppages)
- Some businesses lost 100,000 to 1 million NOK to invoice fraud or BEC scams
- Dam incident costs mainly in incident response and preventive measures (no physical equipment damage)

**Potential Impact:**
Had dam attack gone unnoticed longer or affected power infrastructure, potential damage could have been tens of millions NOK or more.

### Data Breach Numbers

**June 2025:**
- Very few personal records compromised in Norway-specific incidents
- No major breach of Norwegian company announced

**Year to Date (H1 2025):**
- Under **50,000 individuals' data exposed** (mostly from minor incidents earlier in year)
- Low figure internationally, pointing to either:
  - Strong privacy protections
  - Effective security controls
  - Potential underreporting
  - Small market size reducing attractiveness

**Expected Change:**
New DSA law expected to improve reporting transparency and potentially reveal previously unreported incidents.

### User Awareness and Preparedness

**Business Training:**
- **85%** of Norwegian businesses conducted cybersecurity training for employees in past year (NorSIS survey)
- Among highest rates in Europe

**Password Managers:**
- About **30%** of Norwegian adults use password managers
- Growing adoption following public awareness campaigns

**Two-Factor Authentication:**
- Around **65%** use BankID or other 2FA for important accounts
- Strong government digital ID infrastructure supports adoption

**International Comparison:**
Norway shows among highest rates of cyber awareness and preparedness in Europe, reflecting:
- Advanced digital society
- Strong government campaigns
- High education levels
- Cultural emphasis on security

## Impact Assessment

### Critical Findings

**Infrastructure Vulnerability:**
The Bremanger dam incident revealed that even in security-conscious Norway, basic vulnerabilities exist in critical infrastructure. The simplicity of the attack (weak password) is particularly concerning.

**Small Scale, High Awareness:**
Norway's relatively low number of incidents doesn't indicate lack of threats – rather, it reflects:
- Effective defenses by well-resourced organizations
- Smaller attack surface (5.4M population)
- High security awareness
- Strong government-industry cooperation

**Quality Over Quantity:**
While Norway faces fewer attacks than larger nations, the attacks it does face are often:
- High-impact (critical infrastructure)
- State-linked (strategic targeting)
- Persistent (ongoing campaigns)
- Sophisticated (though dam incident was exception)

**Legislative Progress:**
The DSA passage demonstrates Norway's proactive approach, implementing strong requirements before major breach occurs rather than in reaction to catastrophe.

### Positive Developments

**Strong Coordination:**
Seamless cooperation between PST, NSM, NVE, and sectoral authorities demonstrated mature incident response capabilities.

**Rapid Policy Action:**
From dam incident discovery (April 7) to legislative passage (June 23) shows:
- Government responsiveness
- Political will
- Learning from incidents
- Translating lessons into policy

**International Integration:**
Norway's partnerships with NATO and Scandinavian neighbors provide:
- Enhanced threat intelligence
- Coordinated response
- Shared resources
- Collective defense

**Public Engagement:**
High levels of awareness and preparedness among Norwegian citizens and businesses create strong foundation for national cyber resilience.

### Ongoing Challenges

**Russia Threat:**
Persistent low-level activity from pro-Russian groups requires:
- Sustained vigilance
- Continued investment
- International cooperation
- Strategic patience

**SME Vulnerability:**
While large organizations are well-protected, smaller businesses face:
- Resource constraints
- Skills shortages
- Limited budgets
- Attractive targets

**OT Security:**
Dam incident highlighted that operational technology security lags IT security:
- Legacy systems
- Difficult to patch
- Safety concerns limiting security measures
- Requires specialized expertise

**Complacency Risk:**
Low incident numbers could breed complacency:
- Need for sustained investment
- Continuous improvement required
- Threats evolving constantly
- No room for reduced vigilance

## Conclusion

June 2025 represented a turning point for Norwegian cybersecurity. The Bremanger dam incident – though relatively minor in immediate impact – served as a powerful wake-up call about vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure. The government's rapid response, including passage of the Digital Security Act and publication of the NSM framework, demonstrated mature leadership and commitment to cyber resilience.

Norway enters the second half of 2025 with:

**Strengths:**
- Strong legislative framework (DSA)
- Comprehensive national framework (NSM)
- High public awareness (65% using 2FA)
- Effective government coordination
- International partnerships (NATO, Scandinavia)
- Proven incident response capability
- Low successful breach rate

**Challenges:**
- Persistent Russian threat activity
- OT security gaps in critical infrastructure
- SME resource constraints
- Evolving attacker sophistication
- Need for sustained investment
- Implementation of new requirements

**Opportunities:**
- DSA implementation driving security improvements
- Regional leadership in Scandinavia
- Learning from dam incident
- Strong foundation for resilience
- International cooperation expanding

The events of June 2025 – particularly the dam incident and legislative response – will likely be remembered as catalyzing moment that elevated cybersecurity to top-tier national security priority. The combination of practical lessons learned, strong policy framework, international cooperation, and high public engagement positions Norway well to address future cyber threats.

However, success requires sustained commitment. The threats are persistent, evolving, and sophisticated. Norway must continue investing in cybersecurity, implementing the DSA effectively, maintaining international partnerships, and staying vigilant. The relatively calm June 2025 (aside from the dam incident) provided opportunity to strengthen defenses – now comes the work of maintaining and improving that posture in the face of ongoing threats.
